Thursday, April 24, 2025: Market Volatility Continues Amid Trade War Uncertainty

US stocks have experienced significant volatility, particularly since February 2025, primarily due to trade war policies instigated by President Donald Trump. After reaching a record high in February, the S&P 500, a key benchmark for US stock performance, experienced a slump. On Wednesday US stocks soared, but the S&P 500 is still trying to climb out of a slump instigated by President Donald Trump’s trade war. Investors are acutely focused on identifying a potential "bottom" in the market, a point from which sustained recovery can begin. However, as many financial experts point out, definitively predicting the market bottom is inherently challenging.

Historical Context and Market Corrections

To understand the current market dynamics, analysts often refer to historical patterns. Since the end of World War II, the S&P 500 has undergone 24 corrections (a decline of 10% or more from a recent peak). According to research from CFRA, historically, in instances where the S&P 500 entered a correction phase but stopped short of entering a full-blown bear market (a decline of 20% or more), the index typically took around 133 days to bottom out and approximately 113 days to recover to pre-correction levels. The recent market decline, measured from February's peak to the low point of April 8, spanned just 48 days, significantly shorter than the historical average. The time it took for the S&P 500 to confirm that it entered a correction was 22 days, again much faster than the historical average of 77 days. Historically sharp and swift declines have tended to be shallower and shorter-lived compared to slower declines. However, the current correction is unique because it is "manufactured," directly driven by the White House's trade war initiatives. The historical data serves only as a guide, the uncertainty of government policy casts doubt on its reliability.

The "Retest" and Market Bottom Formation

Many analysts anticipate that the market will "retest" the April 8 low before finding a stable bottom. This involves revisiting the previously established low point to gauge investor sentiment and the strength of support at that level. The idea of retesting lows gained prominence after the 1987 market crash. After the dramatic crash of October 19, 1987, the S&P 500 struggled and retested its October low in December of the same year. This retest, which briefly fell below the October low, proved to be the eventual bottom. This "all clear" sign initiated a sustained market recovery. While retesting is not universally necessary in every market slump, analysts consider it likely this year because of the current high levels of uncertainty stemming from geopolitical tensions. Analysts like Ed Yardeni from Yardeni Research suggest the S&P 500 will likely retest its April 8 low and potentially find support there, potentially forming a bottom.

V-Shaped Recovery vs. Sideways Grind

Financial analysts are debating the likely shape of the market's recovery. The last S&P 500 correction in 2023 saw a rapid, "V-shaped" recovery in just 24 days. However, a similar speedy recovery is not guaranteed this year. One analyst at LPL Financial has been hesitant to predict a swift recovery due to the lack of a clear shift from defensive stocks to cyclical stocks, which is considered a key indicator of robust market confidence. Others predict a period of sideways movement with no strong uptrend.

Bearish Sentiment and Technical Indicators

Bearish sentiment (negative views on the market) has been prevalent. The American Association of Individual Investors has tracked bearish sentiment exceeding 50% of respondents for the past eight weeks. There are other, less optimistic signals like the recent "death cross" where the index’s 50-day moving average closed below its 200-day moving average. That can be a sign of more selling to come, according to Stovall.

Investment Strategies for Volatile Markets

Financial advisors recommend investors remain patient and maintain a well-defined investment plan, regardless of short-term market fluctuations. Attempting to time the market (buying low and selling high) is generally discouraged. Staying invested during volatile periods is often a better long-term strategy than selling out of fear. Younger investors with a long-term investment horizon are encouraged to view market corrections as buying opportunities, enabling them to acquire stocks at discounted prices. Investors nearing retirement are advised to diversify their portfolios, including more conservative assets like Treasuries and money market funds, to protect their investments from market downturns. The market's future direction is linked to the clarity and certainty surrounding White House policies. Greater clarity will likely boost investor confidence and lead to more favorable market reactions.
