May 1, 2025: Stocks Surge on Tech Earnings, Trade Concerns Persist

The stock market experienced a significant upswing on Thursday, May 1, 2025, driven primarily by strong quarterly earnings from major technology companies like Microsoft and Meta Platforms. This surge in tech stocks helped to alleviate concerns about a potential slowdown in artificial intelligence (AI) progress amidst broader economic uncertainties. However, lingering anxieties surrounding international trade, particularly President Donald Trump's tariff policies, continue to cast a shadow on the market's overall outlook.

Market Performance

Stocks closed higher across the board, with the Nasdaq Composite leading the charge due to the robust performance of tech giants. The S&P 500 also saw substantial gains, reflecting the broad positive sentiment spurred by the earnings reports. The Dow Jones Industrial Average also climbed, contributing to what marked a positive start to May after a volatile April.

The market's positive reaction to tech earnings suggests that investors believe the AI sector remains resilient, despite concerns about a possible economic downturn. Microsoft's earnings, in particular, were driven by its Azure cloud business, signaling strong demand for cloud computing and AI services. Meta's results also exceeded expectations, with CEO Mark Zuckerberg expressing confidence in the company's ability to navigate macroeconomic uncertainties. Their financial performance alleviated concern from experts like Jed Ellerbroek, portfolio manager at Argent Capital Management, that the AI infrastructure progress is 'a lot less impacted than investors currently believe'.

Economic Data and Analysis

While the strong tech earnings fueled a rally, economic data released this week painted a mixed picture. Weekly jobless claims surged to 241,000, higher than anticipated, raising concerns about the strength of the labor market. This came on the heels of a disappointing gross domestic product (GDP) report for the first quarter, which showed the U.S. economy contracting at an annualized rate of 0.4%.

This contraction marked the first decline in GDP since 2022, and was attributed to a surge in imports as businesses front-loaded inventory ahead of President Trump's tariffs, as well as a slowdown in consumer spending and government spending. The GDP decline briefly whipsawed investors, causing concern that the US economy was much weaker than previous thought. In April, the US also saw a pull back in hiring that was contributed to what the report called a 'difficult' environment defined by 'unease' among businesses.

The Institute for Supply Management (ISM) manufacturing index, however, offered a glimmer of hope, coming in better than expected despite the looming threat of tariffs. This suggested that factory activity remains relatively strong, although survey respondents did express concerns about higher prices and profitability constraints due to tariffs.

Trade Tensions and Tariff Policies

Persistent concerns about trade tensions and tariff policies continue to weigh on market sentiment. President Trump's "reciprocal" tariff announcement earlier in April triggered a steep market sell-off, and uncertainty surrounding the outlook for trade agreements remains high. Piper Sandler analysts suggest that investors shouldn't get their hopes up regarding new trade agreements, stating 'We believe these 'deals' are likely to disappoint'. They anticipated ongoing discussions.

Several companies, including General Motors and Caterpillar, have already warned about the potential negative impact of tariffs on their earnings. General Motors cut its full-year outlook, expecting a tariff-related hit of at least $4 billion. Eli Lilly, a large drugmaker, also lowered its profit outlook following a deal. The company's stock, LLY, closed nearly 11% lower as a result.

Company News and Developments

In other company news, Kohl's fired its CEO, Ashley Buchanan, due to a personal relationship with a vendor and 'highly unusual terms' that involved deals the company said Buchanan advocated. Tesla shares rose after the electric vehicle maker denied a report that its board was searching for a replacement for CEO Elon Musk. Vaccine stocks fell amid reports that the FDA plans to require placebo-controlled trials for updated vaccine boosters.

Analyst Outlook

Despite the economic uncertainties, some analysts remain optimistic about the market's prospects. UBS suggested that the market may return better-than-average gains in the coming months, citing historical trends that show above-average returns when investors are pessimistic and fearful. CFRA Research's Sam Stovall believes that the worst of the current correction is likely behind us, and that the market will climb back up to its February high.

However, JPMorgan warned that tariff uncertainty has spurred a massive divergence between sentiment and growth, and that it is unclear whether the drop in sentiment is an overreaction or that growth drivers are being overstated by tariff-related front-loading.

Looking Ahead

The market's near-term performance will likely depend on developments related to trade negotiations, as well as upcoming economic data releases, including April's nonfarm payrolls report. Investors will also be closely watching earnings reports from companies like Apple and Amazon, to assess the impact of tariffs and macroeconomic conditions on their businesses.
